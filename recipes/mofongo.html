Puerto Rican Mofongo

Description

Mofongo is a traditional Puerto Rican dish made primarily from fried green plantains that are mashed together with garlic, olive oil, and chicharrón (fried pork skin). The result is a savory, hearty mound of plantains that can be served on its own or paired with proteins like shrimp, chicken, or beef, often accompanied by a rich broth or sauce. Its origins are rooted in African culinary traditions brought to the Caribbean, and over time it has become a defining comfort food of Puerto Rican cuisine, celebrated for its bold flavor and satisfying texture.

At its core, mofongo highlights the versatility of the plantain, transforming it into a dish that balances starchy, garlicky, and meaty flavors. While the traditional preparation uses a wooden mortar and pestle (pilón), variations abound—some add cheese, seafood, or vegetables, while others replace pork cracklings with bacon or keep it vegetarian. Regardless of the spin, mofongo remains a beloved symbol of Puerto Rican culinary identity, often enjoyed both in casual family meals and festive gatherings.

Ingredients

  • 3–4 green plantains, peeled and sliced into chunks
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • ½ cup chicharrón (or bacon as substitute)
  • 3–4 tbsp olive oil or butter
  • Salt to taste
  • Optional: chicken or shrimp broth for serving

Steps

  1. Heat oil in a skillet and fry the plantain pieces until golden and tender. Remove and drain.
  2. In a mortar and pestle (or sturdy bowl), mash the garlic with olive oil, then add fried plantains and chicharrón. Pound until the mixture is cohesive but still chunky. Season with salt.
  3. Shape into a mound or ball and serve immediately, either with broth poured over or alongside a protein of choice.
